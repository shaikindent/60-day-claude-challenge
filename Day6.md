# What I Learned Running My Own Resume Through ATS-Style Prompting

I used Claude to stress-test my resume the way an Applicant Tracking System — and a human reader skimming it for 8 seconds — actually would. A few of the lessons surprised me enough to share.

---

## 1. Sixteen years of leadership, zero numbers

That was the single biggest finding. Every bullet described *scope* (six hospitals, eleven hospitals, 85 facilities) but not *impact*. ATS systems and recruiters are both pattern-matching for outcomes, not duties.

**Before:**
> Maintains the system-wide Quality, Patient Safety, and Risk Management programs for the ADH Region, including data collection, aggregation, and analysis...

**After (technique, not invented numbers):**
> Maintains system-wide Quality, Patient Safety, and Risk Management programs across [X] facilities — reporting [specific metric, e.g., incident rate trend, accreditation pass rate, cost avoidance] to the Corporate Office and CEO.

The fix isn't "add buzzwords." It's going back through your own records and finding the three or four numbers that already exist — survey scores, error-rate trends, readmission deltas — and inserting them where the duty currently sits unsupported.

---

## 2. Your title is doing less work than your achievements

A resume titled "Chief Quality Officer" reads identically whether the reader is a hospital board, an accreditation body, or an AI investor. ATS keyword matching and human skimming both reward *specificity in the first 3 lines*, not just the job title.

**Before (generic profile opener):**
> Performance-driven quality and patient safety professional with over sixteen years of experience...

**After:**
> Enterprise Chief Quality Officer with 16+ years leading JCI accreditation, high-reliability systems, and AI-enabled quality analytics across multi-hospital networks in the UAE.

Same facts. The second version front-loads the exact phrases a search committee or ATS query is likely to contain: "JCI," "high-reliability," "multi-hospital," "enterprise." That's not keyword stuffing — it's just stating what's already true, earlier.

---

## 3. Your most differentiating credential was buried on page 2

JCI 8th Edition Technical Advisory Panel membership is genuinely rare — most CQOs implement standards; very few help write them. On the original resume it appeared as a small caption under a book image, with equal visual weight to a 2014 newsletter article.

**Lesson:** ATS parsing and human attention both work the same way — they weight what's *near the top* and in a *recognizable field* (like "Certifications" or "Credentials") far more than what's buried in a sidebar graphic. If something is rare, it needs its own line near your name, not a caption.

---

## 4. Current ventures often go unmentioned — and that's a silent gap

My resume had no reference to Qonomics anywhere, despite it being a live, current focus. An ATS — and a human — both read an undated resume as "this is the complete current picture." Anything you're building right now that isn't on the page effectively doesn't exist to the reader.

**Lesson:** Treat your resume like a changelog, not a monument. If your focus has shifted toward AI-finance integration in quality, the resume should say so in the present tense, even briefly — not require the reader to infer it from your publication titles.

---

## 5. A 40-line publication list reads as noise, not authority

Exhaustive lists feel like proof of rigor, but both ATS parsers and human readers lose the signal in the volume. ~45 peer-reviewed articles is a strong credential — as a *count*, stated once. As 15 individually italicized citations, it becomes wallpaper.

**Before:** Full citation list with co-authors, DOIs, journal volume/issue for every paper.

**After:** "Peer-reviewed researcher (Web of Science, ORCID) — 45+ published articles; editorial board member, 2 journals. Selected publications available on request / LinkedIn."

Save the full list for an academic CV. Save the resume for the headline number.

---

## The meta-lesson

ATS optimization isn't really about beating a robot. It's about **forcing the same discipline you'd apply to a quality dashboard onto your own career data** — separating signal from noise, leading with outcomes, and not assuming the reader will connect dots you haven't drawn for them. Ironically, the exercise itself was a small case study in the kind of "make the data legible" work I do for a living.
